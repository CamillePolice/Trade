Rules

For this competition you will have to write a bot that trades currencies on the crypto market. The bot starts with $1000,- and the goal is to maximize the value of all owned currencies at the end of the game.

Getting started

There is a training set with chart data available to create your both with. It is included in the Java starterbot, but you can also download it here. This is also the chart data your bot will run against in the test match, that will automatically play when you upload a bot revision. The test match is for your own eyes only and does not count towards the score on the leaderboard. When you are happy with the performance of your bot, you can press ASSESS NOW on a bot revision to play a match against a secret data set. The highest assessment score of all your bot revisions will automatically be visible on the leaderboard.

The chart data

Currently there are 3 different currencies available.

  • USDT (Tether, or the crypto version of US Dollar)
  • BTC (Bitcoin)
  • ETH (Ether)

Between these three currencies we have all trading pairs, which results in three available charts.

  • USDT_BTC
  • USDT_ETH
  • BTC_ETH

Your bot will receive chart data in the form of 30-minute candles as below (CSV notation). In the game your bot gets all candles at a certain timestamp at the same time (see API and Examples).

pair,date,high,low,open,close,volume
USDT_ETH,1516147200,1090.1676815,1022.16791604,1023.1,1029.99999994,1389783.7868468

The first line shows the column names, the second line is an example of a candle. Below you'll find the description of each column you see here.

Figure 1: Candles and volume.
  • pair The chart to which this candle belongs.
  • date Unix timestamp, which represents a certain date and time.
  • high The highest price traded in this candle.
  • low The lowest price traded in this candle.
  • open The opening price of this candle.
  • close The closing price of this candle.
  • volume The total volume that has been traded in this candle.

In Figure 1 you can see how candles are represented in the graphs of the visualizer. When the closing price is higher than the opening price, the candle is green. When the closing price is lower than the opening price, the candle is red. In the visualizer you can additionally see the graphs for the total value and the amount (stacks) of each currency, namely USDT, BTC, and ETH.

In the game the bot will receive a certain amount of candles beforehand, currently this is one week worth of candle data. After that, each time new candle data is given, the engine will ask to bot which orders it wants to place.

Buying and selling

When the engine asks the bot for an action, the bot can place up to one buy or sell order for each of the trading pairs. To do nothing at the current timestamp, the bot can also pass.

On a buy order, the first symbol is the currency that is paid with, the second is the currency that is received. The amount specifies how much to buy of the second symbol. So for example buy USDT_ETH 1 means the bot wants to buy 1 ETH. The cost would be 1 * [current USDT_ETH price] in USDT.

On a sell order, the first symbol is the currency that is received, the second symbol is the currency that is sold. The amount specifies how much to sell of the second symbol. sell USDT_ETH 1 means the bot wants to sell 1 ETH in this case. The bot will receive 1 * [current USDT_ETH price] - [fee] in USDT if the order succeeds.

Just like on a real trading platform, your bot will have to pay a transaction fee for each order it places. Currently the bot pays a 0.2% transaction fee, so when buying 1 ETH, the bot will actually receive 0,998 ETH.

General rules and technicalities

This section contains some rules and techinalities that apply to all games and competitions accross the Riddles.io platform.

Timebank

The timebank we use is meant to allow for some additional thinking time when needed. Your bot gets a maximally filled timebank when the game starts (usually 10 seconds). Each time an action is requested, the timebank will be increased with the amount given in the time_per_move setting (usually 100 - 500ms), but the timebank can never exceed its maximum. The time your bot used to respond to the request is deducted from the timebank. So if your bot sometimes needs more time than the time_per_move, it can exhaust the timebank.

Game boards and fields

In many of our competitions, the game is played on a board or a field that is represented on a grid. The origin of the grid is always in the top-left corner, as is usual in computer science. This means x=0, y=0 is the top-left corner of the grid, x=9, y=9 is the bottom-right corner of a grid with height and width 10.

The string representation of the board or field always separates the cells in grid with a comma and returns them per row, from top-left to bottom-right.

Upload and activate your bot

To upload your bot, create a .zip file of the source files and upload it on the competition pages. For Java specifically, make sure your file/folder structure is the same as the classpaths in your code. Activate a bot revision on the overview page and it will appear on the leaderboard and start to play ranked matches automatically against the other activated bots.
